## What it shows

The circle of fifths arranges all twelve major keys (and their relative minors) in a
circle, each one a perfect fifth above the last: C, G, D, A, E, B, F#/Gb, Db, Ab,
Eb, Bb, F, and back to C. Moving clockwise adds one sharp per step; moving
counter-clockwise (by fifths downward, i.e. by fourths) adds one flat per step. It
is simultaneously a key-signature reference (how many sharps or flats a key has,
read off by counting steps from C) and a map of harmonic relatedness — keys that sit
next to each other on the circle share six of their seven notes and feel like close,
easy modulations, while keys on opposite sides sound the most distant from one
another.

## Why guitarists use it

Two practical uses come up constantly. First, the V and IV chords of any key are
its immediate neighbors on the circle (one step clockwise = V, one step
counter-clockwise = IV), so the circle is a fast way to find the dominant and
subdominant of any key without spelling out the scale. Second, many common chord
progressions are literally movement around the circle: a ii-V-I in C (Dm-G-C) is
three consecutive clockwise steps landing back on the tonic, and the descending
"circle progression" used in countless pop and jazz songs is nothing more than
walking backward around the circle one fifth at a time. Recognizing that pattern
makes it much faster to predict where a progression is "going" by ear.
