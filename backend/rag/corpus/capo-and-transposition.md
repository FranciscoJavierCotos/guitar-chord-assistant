## What a capo does

A capo clamps across all six strings at a chosen fret, acting like a movable nut and
raising the pitch of every open string by one half step per fret. Playing the exact
same chord shapes with a capo on therefore produces different, higher-pitched
chords without changing a single finger position — a capo on fret 2 turns an
open-position G shape into an actual A chord, for instance. This lets a guitarist
keep using easy, familiar open-chord shapes while still landing on whatever real
key the song or singer needs, which is the main reason capos are so common in
singer-songwriter and folk contexts.

## Transposition with a capo

To transpose a progression down to easier open shapes: count how many frets up the
desired key is from a comfortable open-chord key, place the capo there, and play the
open-chord version of the lower key. For example, a song in Ab major is awkward in
open position, but capo 1 plus playing the open G-shaped chords (G, C, D, Em...)
sounds exactly like Ab, G#m, C#, etc. — because capo-1 raises every shape by one
half step. The general rule: real sounding key = shape's key + capo fret (in half
steps). This is also why two guitarists can play "the same song" with completely
different capo positions and shapes and still sound identical together, as long as
shape-key-plus-capo-fret adds up to the same real key for both.
