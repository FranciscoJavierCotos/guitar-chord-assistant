## The major scale formula

Every major scale follows the same interval pattern starting from its tonic: whole,
whole, half, whole, whole, whole, half (W-W-H-W-W-W-H). Applying that pattern to C
gives the white-key scale C-D-E-F-G-A-B-C with no sharps or flats, which is why C
major is the usual starting point for teaching theory. Apply the same pattern to G
and you get G-A-B-C-D-E-F#-G — one sharp. The pattern never changes; only the
starting note and the resulting accidentals do. This is the single most useful fact
in tonal theory: once a guitarist has the W-W-H-W-W-W-H pattern memorized, they can
derive any major scale and, from it, every diatonic chord in that key.

## Key signatures

A key signature is the fixed set of sharps or flats that belong to a key, written
once at the start of a piece rather than next to every note. Key signatures follow a
predictable order: sharp keys add sharps in the order F#-C#-G#-D#-A#-E#-B#, flat
keys add flats in the order Bb-Eb-Ab-Db-Gb-Cb-Fb — each new key signature is the
previous one plus one more accidental in that fixed sequence. Knowing a song's key
signature immediately tells a guitarist which notes are "in key" and therefore which
frets are safe to target when improvising a fill or harmonizing a melody, without
having to spell the scale out degree by degree every time.
