## The seven diatonic triads

Stacking thirds on top of each note of a major scale, using only notes already in
that scale, produces seven triads with a fixed quality pattern: I and IV and V are
major; ii, iii, and vi are minor; vii° is diminished. In the key of C that's
C-Dm-Em-F-G-Am-Bdim. This pattern is the same in every major key, which is why
knowing "the I, IV, and V are major; ii, iii, vi are minor; vii is diminished" lets
a guitarist build the full chord set for any key from the scale alone, without
memorizing each key's chords separately.

## Function: tonic, subdominant, dominant

Each diatonic chord also has a harmonic function — a role in creating and releasing
tension, grouped into three families. Tonic chords (I, vi, and to a lesser extent
iii) sound stable and "at rest." Subdominant chords (IV, ii) create mild movement
away from rest, often described as "leaving home." Dominant chords (V, vii°)
create the strongest pull back to the tonic, largely because they contain the
leading tone (scale degree 7) a half step below the tonic. A typical harmonic
journey is tonic → subdominant → dominant → tonic, and most familiar progressions
are variations on that shape. Substituting a chord of the same function for another
(e.g. vi for I, since they share two notes) is a standard way to vary a progression
while keeping its underlying harmonic motion intact.
