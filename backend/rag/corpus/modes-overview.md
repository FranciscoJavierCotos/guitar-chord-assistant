## The seven modes of the major scale

A mode is the major scale's notes reordered to start from a different degree, which
shifts the pattern of whole and half steps and gives each mode a distinct
character. In order from the 1st degree: Ionian (the major scale itself, bright),
Dorian (minor with a raised 6th, a "cool" minor used heavily in funk and rock),
Phrygian (minor with a flattened 2nd, dark and Spanish-flamenco-flavored), Lydian
(major with a raised 4th, dreamy/floating), Mixolydian (major with a flattened 7th,
bluesy/rock-and-roll), Aeolian (the natural minor scale), and Locrian (diminished
and unstable, rarely used as a tonal center). Crucially, all seven modes of, say, C
major use exactly the same seven notes as C major itself — what changes is which
note acts as the tonic (the "home" the ear gravitates to), not the pitch content.

## Hearing and using modes on guitar

The fastest way to actually hear a mode's character is to play it over a static
drone or vamp on its own tonic, rather than just running the major scale shape
starting on a different fret — without a clear tonal center the "modal flavor"
doesn't register. For example, playing a D-to-D scale using only the notes of C
major (D Dorian) over a held D power chord makes the raised 6th (B) stand out as
the mode's signature color tone. Guitarists most often invoke modes by name when
describing a vamp's flavor (e.g. "that riff is in E Phrygian") or when choosing
notes to bend into during a solo that sit outside the plain major/minor sound while
staying diatonic to the underlying key.
