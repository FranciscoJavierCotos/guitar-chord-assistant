## I-IV-V and I-V-vi-IV

I-IV-V is the backbone of blues, early rock and roll, and folk: three major chords a
fourth and a fifth above the tonic, all drawn from the same key, giving a song that
moves and resolves without ever leaving "home" harmonically. In C that's C-F-G. I-V-
vi-IV swaps in the relative minor for variety — in C, C-G-Am-F — and is sometimes
called the "pop-punk progression" or "four chords" progression because of how often
it appears across decades of mainstream pop; the vi chord briefly darkens the mood
before IV brings it back toward resolution.

## ii-V-I and the 12-bar blues

ii-V-I is the defining cadence of jazz harmony: the ii chord (subdominant function)
sets up the V chord (dominant function, maximum tension via its leading tone), which
resolves to I (tonic, rest). In C that's Dm-G-C. Because it expresses a complete
tension-and-release arc in just three chords, ii-V-I segments are stitched together
to form entire jazz standards, often modulating to a new key for the next ii-V-I.
The 12-bar blues is a longer, fixed form built almost entirely from I, IV, and V
over twelve bars: four bars of I, two bars of IV, two bars of I, then one bar each
of V, IV, I, and a turnaround back to V (or I) — the same three chords as the basic
I-IV-V, but arranged into a specific, recognizable rhythmic template that blues,
rock, and early jazz musicians use as a shared form to improvise over.
