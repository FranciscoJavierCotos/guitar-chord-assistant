## The down-up motion and the beat

Strumming technique is built on a continuous, metronomic down-up motion of the
picking hand that keeps time even on beats where no string is actually struck —
downstrokes naturally fall on the beat and upstrokes on the off-beat ("and")
between beats. Keeping that hand moving constantly, and simply choosing to mute or
skip a stroke rather than stopping the motion, is what lets a guitarist play
syncopated patterns cleanly and stay locked to tempo; stopping and restarting the
arm to "place" each strum individually is the most common cause of rhythm drifting
when a beginner first tries a new pattern.

## Dynamics, muting, and groove

A strumming pattern is more than which strings get hit — dynamics (hitting some
strums harder, others softer) and muting (resting the strumming-hand palm or
fretting-hand fingers on the strings to create a short, percussive "chuck" instead
of a ringing chord) are what give a pattern its groove and genre feel. A reggae
"skank" emphasizes short, muted upstrokes on the off-beat; a folk strum often
accents beat 1 and lets chords ring; funk rhythm guitar leans heavily on tight,
muted 16th-note "chucks." Internalizing a steady subdivision (counting "1-and-2-
and-3-and-4-and") before adding any muting or accents is the standard order of
learning, because it's far easier to selectively skip or mute strokes within a
pattern you already feel steadily than to build steadiness and syncopation at the
same time.
