## Right-hand finger assignment

Fingerpicking (also called fingerstyle) plucks individual strings with the picking
hand's fingers and thumb instead of strumming with a pick. The standard convention
assigns the thumb (p) to the bass strings (low E, A, D) and the index (i), middle
(m), and ring (a) fingers to the treble strings (G, B, high e) respectively, usually
one finger per string. Keeping that assignment consistent — rather than letting any
finger grab any string — is what allows a pattern to become automatic with
practice, the same way fixed left-hand fingering lets a chord shape become muscle
memory.

## Travis picking and arpeggiated patterns

The most common fingerpicking pattern in folk and country guitar is Travis picking:
the thumb alternates steadily between two bass strings (typically the root and the
5th of the current chord) on the beat, independent of whatever the fingers are
doing, while the fingers pick out a melody or arpeggio on top. That thumb
independence — keeping a steady alternating bass going underneath changing finger
patterns — is the technical skill that takes longest to build and is what separates
basic fingerpicking from Travis picking specifically. Simpler arpeggiated patterns
skip the alternating-bass requirement and just pick through a chord's notes in a
fixed order (e.g. p-i-m-a or p-m-i-a), which is the usual starting point before
attempting independent thumb patterns, since it only requires learning one
repeating motion rather than coordinating two independent ones.
