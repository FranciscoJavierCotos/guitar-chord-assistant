## Open chords

Open chords use at least one unfretted ("open") string ringing alongside fretted
notes, typically played in the first three frets. They're the first chords most
beginners learn — C, G, D, Em, Am, and similar shapes — because they require less
finger independence and strength than barre chords, and the open strings give them a
characteristically bright, resonant sound that's hard to fully replicate higher up
the neck. Their main limitation is that each open-chord shape is tied to a specific
key (a "C shape" can't simply slide up the neck and remain a usable chord, because
the open strings stop matching the shape), so a song that needs many different
chords in an awkward key may not have convenient open-chord options for all of them.

## Barre chords

A barre chord uses one finger (usually the index) pressed flat across multiple
strings to substitute for the nut, with the rest of the hand forming a moveable
shape on top — most often built from the open E or A shapes. Because no string is
left open, the entire shape can slide to any fret and the same finger pattern
produces a different chord at every position, which is what makes barre chords
essential for playing in keys with few convenient open-chord options (like Bb or
F#) and for quickly transposing a song. The trade-off is physical: barring cleanly
takes real hand strength and finger conditioning that beginners typically need
weeks of practice to build, and a poorly-placed or under-pressured barre finger is
the most common cause of buzzing or muted strings for new guitarists.
