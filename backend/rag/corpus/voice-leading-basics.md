## What voice leading is

Voice leading is the practice of moving between chords using the smallest, smoothest
possible motion in each individual note ("voice"), rather than jumping every note to
the next chord's root-position shape. Between many adjacent diatonic chords, one or
two notes can stay exactly where they are (a "common tone") while the rest move by
just a step or two — for example C major and A minor share two of their three notes,
so moving between them barely requires the ear (or the fretting hand) to travel. Good
voice leading is a big part of why some chord changes feel effortless and connected
while others feel like a "jump," even when both pairs are equally valid harmonically.

## Why it matters on guitar specifically

On guitar, voice leading has a very physical payoff: choosing chord voicings (not
just chord names) that share fingers or stay in the same fretboard region makes
transitions faster and smoother to play, not just smoother to hear. A guitarist
moving from G to Em, for instance, can keep the same two fretted notes and only
adjust one finger, instead of releasing the whole shape and re-gripping a fresh one
three frets away. This is also the principle behind choosing inversions or alternate
voicings of a chord (the same chord with a different note on the bottom, or played
higher up the neck) specifically to minimize hand movement across a whole
progression, which is standard practice for arranging a song to sound — and feel —
connected rather than mechanical.
