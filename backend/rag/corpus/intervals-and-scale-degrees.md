## What an interval is

An interval is the distance between two pitches, measured in steps. On guitar the
smallest interval is a half step — one fret. A whole step is two frets. Naming an
interval combines a quality (major, minor, perfect, augmented, diminished) with a
number (2nd, 3rd, 4th, ...) counted from the lower note. The perfect intervals
(unison, 4th, 5th, octave) sound stable and consonant; major/minor 3rds and 6ths
sound warm; major/minor 2nds and 7ths sound tense and want to resolve. Recognizing
intervals by ear and by fretboard shape — a major 3rd is always two strings and one
fret away in standard tuning's adjacent-string shapes once you account for the G–B
string's tuning quirk — is the foundation for building chords and reading any scale
diagram.

## Scale degrees and Roman numerals

A scale degree is a note's position within a key, numbered 1 through 7 starting on
the tonic. Scale degrees have traditional names: tonic (1), supertonic (2), mediant
(3), subdominant (4), dominant (5), submediant (6), leading tone (7). Roman numerals
describe chords built on each degree — uppercase for major, lowercase for minor,
e.g. I–ii–iii–IV–V–vi–vii° in a major key — which is why "the I–V–vi–IV progression"
means the same shape in any key: it names a relationship between scale degrees, not
specific notes. Thinking in scale degrees rather than absolute note names is what
lets a guitarist transpose a song to a more comfortable key without re-deriving the
progression from scratch.
